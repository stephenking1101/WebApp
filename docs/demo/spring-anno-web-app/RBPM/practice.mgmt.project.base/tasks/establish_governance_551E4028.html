Responsibility matrix guidelines

The responsibility matrix details the relationships between roles and deliverables. The rows on the matrix show the standard list of deliverables from each of the various project stages. The column titles show the various roles involved in the different aspects of the project.

The action to be performed against the deliverable by the role is shown at the intersections between the two. There are four options for this:

  • Responsible (R) - This shows that the individual detailed by role produces, or is responsible for producing, the deliverable.
  • Approves / Signs Off (A) - This shows that the individual detailed by role is responsible for approving / signing off the deliverable.
  • Contributes (C) - This shows that role provides input to the deliverable.
  • Informed (I) - This shows that the individual detailed by role is a reviewer of the deliverable.

Only roles defined in the role overviews should be used. Responsibility Matrix or RACI Template can be accessed here.

Establish Change Control process

The Change Control process ensures that due consideration is given to the impact of requirement change (business or technical) on the project and that approved changes are made within a project in a consistent manner.

Procedure

  1. Identify the driving project constraint. For example, scope, budget, schedule or quality). While managing the changes, the driving constraint will have overriding consideration
  2. Determine the roles and responsibilities for managing the changes. Typical roles include - Change Control Board, Change Evaluator etc.
  3. Establish the evaluation process. See Change Control.
  4. Establish guidelines for communicating changes to the project team
Define Risk Management Plan

Risk Management Planning is conducted to define the process of how to engage and oversee risk management activities for a project. Risk Management planning is an important part of project management. Having a plan on how to manage risk allows project teams to proactively prepare and plan for risks rather than being in a reactive mode should a risk materialise to an issue. Early engagement of Risk Management planning increases the possibility of success of all risk management activities and processes especially if the process definition was created with input and buy-in from the project manager and key project stakeholders.

Based on the discussion with Project Steering Committee and key stakeholders, document the following:

  • Methodology: Methodology describes how the Risk Management processes will be performed, the tools which will be utilised, and the data source for handling risk.
  • Roles and responsibilities: Roles and responsibilities matrix identifies the lead, support, and assigns people to the roles clarifying their responsibility and accountability.
  • Timing: Timing clarifies the frequency of the risk management process and schedules some risk management activities in the project schedule. Without timely monitoring and response, risks can easily escalate into negative events or become missed opportunities because you failed to exploit them.
  • Risk categories: Risk categories are potential causes of risk included in the Risk Management Plan for use during the Risk Identification and Risk Analysis processes..
  • Definitions of risk probability and impact: Agreeing on standard definitions (for probability: rare, unlikely, often, likely, or expected; for impact: insignificant, minor, moderate, major, or massive) helps to ensure that everyone is communicating on the same wavelength. Definitions are included in the Risk Management Plan for later use during Risk Analysis.
  • Probability and impact matrix: The probability and impact matrix assists in determining the absolute risk score by combining the two dimensions of a risk: its probability of occurrence, and its impact on objectives.
  • Revised stakeholder tolerances: As discussions become more specific during planning meetings about actual risks and actual costs, schedules, scope, objectives, and quality criteria, you get a better idea of your stakeholders' tolerance for risk than you had at the start of the process.
  • Reporting formats: Reporting formats depict the content and format of the risk register. The Risk Register is a document on which you record identified risks and their characteristics (this information should be documented in the corporate Project Management Tool, if available).
  • Tracking: Tracking describes how and when risk information will be documented and reviewed for the benefit of current project, future needs, and lessons learned. Tracking also specifies whether risk management processes will be audited.
Define Artifact (Configuration) Management Plan

The Configuration Management process involves controlling the numerous artefacts produced by the many people working on a project. The purpose is to avoid costly confusion and ensure that resultant artefacts are not in conflict due to the following problems:

  • Simultaneous update to the same artefact by more than one team member.
  • Limited notification – when a problem is fixed in artefacts shared by several team members, and some of them are not notified of the change.
  • Multiple versions – one may be in production while another is in development. Fixes to one version need to be propagated to the others as appropriate in a controlled way, otherwise, confusion can arise leading to costly fixes and re-work.

Procedure

To plan project configuration control:

  1. Establish project configuration management policies.

    When defining policies, consideration may be given to a number of aspects including configuration identification practices, baselining practices, archiving practices, monitoring and reporting configuration status practices, and level of traceability. see: Configuration Management Policy Guidelines, below.

  2. Document the policy information in the Configuration Management Plan.
  3. Have the Plan reviewed by the major stakeholders and the affected groups to ensure the plan is understood and adopted by such stakeholders.

Guidelines

Archiving

Project master documents should be regularly backed-up and transferred to designated storage sites for re-use or contingency purpose.

Traceability

Traceability is the ability to trace a project element to other related project elements, especially those related to requirements. Project elements involved in traceability are called traceability items. Typical traceability items include requirements, analysis and design model elements, test artefacts, and end-user support documentation and training material.
