Task: Control Project

Purpose

Monitoring and recording results of project execution to assess performance and recommend necessary changes.

Steps

Control Risks

Procedure

  1. Review risks identified earlier in the Project, comparing them to the risk register to identify:
    • any ineffectiveness of the response strategy/actions in addressing the risks, for example, has the probability or impact been reduced or has the contingency actions taken
    • any new risks that may have arisen and determine their risk levels, for example:
      • assumptions which have resolved unfavourably
      • constraints which have become a potential hazard to the project
      • tasks on the project plan which did not go well.
    • any technical risks where standard best practices are not followed, such as manually setting the date and time in a system.
    • any legislative risk, such as Daylight Saving Time, that will be changing in any stakeholder country
    • any existing risks where risk levels have changed; determine the new levels of probability, impact, and priority.
    • any risks that can be closed (for example, when the risk has been reduced to such a low probability or impact level that it has practically no impact on the project or programme). When considering whether to close a risk, think about whether it is possible for the risk to occur again. If it is, then do not close it.
  2. Evaluate impact of realised risks
  3. Review and update the risk register with any new risk that may be identified.
  4. Report status of risk management activities in regular project status reports
Control Issues

Procedure

  1. Assign a priority to identified issues and record them in the issue log
  2. Determine how issues are to be resolved and assign them for resolution
  3. Escalate issues as required by escalation rules
  4. Monitor issue resolution status
  5. Report status of issue management activities in regular project status reports
Control Changes
  • Monitor and control integration of changes into project documents
  • Determine if the change request requires special attention and, if so, change evaluators and approvers accordingly. Otherwise follow, Change Control.
  • Report status of change control activities in regular project status reports
Control Quality

Quality Control consists of the execution of project tasks according to the pre-agreed standards. This includes both the generic Group standards, and any project specific controls which may have been developed to meet a specific quality need for the project. The purpose of Quality Control is to identify non conformances and defects and ensure they are corrected so that defect-free work-products or deliverables will be produced.

Procedure

There is no routine procedure for quality control. It involves monitoring specific project results to determine if they comply with relevant quality standards and identifying ways to eliminate causes of unsatisfactory performance. It is applicable throughout the project. Information about both planned and expected results from the project plan should be available with the information about the actual results. Quality tools and techniques are used to assist in performing these activities (see Tools and techniques Guidelines below). 

Quality Control Outputs

The outputs from quality control include:

Quality improvement

Quality Improvement includes taking actions to increase the effectiveness and efficiency of the project to provide added benefits to the project stakeholders.

Acceptance decisions

The documents or deliverables inspected may be accepted or rejected. Rejected documents or deliverables may require rework.

Rework

Rework is the action to bring the defective or nonconforming document or deliverable into compliance with the requirements or specifications. Rework, especially unanticipated rework, is a frequent cause of project overruns in most application areas.

Completed checklists

When checklists are used to perform a given set of activities or tasks, or to verify that a given set of activities or tasks has been performed, the completed checklist may become a part of the project's records

Process adjustments

Process adjustments may involve immediate corrective and preventive actions as a result of quality control measurements.

Tools and techniques guidelines

Inspection

Inspection includes actions such as measuring, examining, and testing, undertaken to determine whether results conform to requirements. Inspections may be conducted at any level. For example, the results for a single activity may be inspected or the final product of the project may be inspected. Inspections are variously called reviews, product reviews and audits.

Control charts

A control chart is a graphical representation over time of a process. It is used to determine if the process is "in control". For example are the differences in the results created by random variations or are unusual events occurring whose causes must be identified and corrected.

Pareto diagrams

A Pareto diagram is a histogram ordered by the frequency of occurrence, which shows how many results were generated by the type or category of the identified cause. Rank ordering is used to provide corrective action. Action will be taken to fix first the problems that are causing the highest number of defects. This is commonly referred as the 80/20 Principle, where 80 percent of the problems are due to 20 percent of the causes.

Statistical sampling

Statistical sampling involves choosing part of a population of interest for inspection.

Appropriate sampling can reduce the cost of quality control.

Flowcharting

Flowcharting is used in quality control to help analyse how problems occur.

Trend analysis

Trend Analysis involves using mathematical techniques to forecast future outcomes based on historical results.

It is often used to monitor:

Technical Performance – how many defects have been identified and how many remain uncorrected.

Cost and Schedule performance – how many activities per period were completed with in the significant variations.

Key Considerations

Risks 

Where risks have significantly increased, a full Project Review should be requested.

Risk review meetings must be scheduled regularly throughout the project lifecycle following the schedule determined in the Risk Management Plan, and may begin as soon as the Risk Register has been initially created. The frequency is dependent on the nature and timeline of the project.

Decide in advance if the risk review meeting is to be the only forum for raising, closing and altering risks.

Issues

Monitor the status of an issue and the progress of the contingency actions or workarounds closely and report to project stakeholders until closure.

Executing risk strategy involves implementing risk response strategies in the project plan, monitoring and reacting to risk triggers, symptoms, indicators and residual risks. Several tools can be used to assist in executing a risk strategy:

  • Technical performance measurements
  • Status reports and meetings
  • Early warning signs
  • Risk based project review
  • Risk and issue log.
